# Argentina — Internal Tactics Dossier (Staff Only)

**Classification:** Internal squad-console analysis. Not for public distribution.
**Manager:** Lionel Scaloni
**Context:** 2026 FIFA World Cup cycle. Most recent match: Argentina 3–2 Egypt (2026-07-07, World Cup).

## Scaloni's Managerial Philosophy

Scaloni took over in 2018 in the wreckage of a dysfunctional Sampaoli cycle and rebuilt the national team around three pillars: defensive solidity first, collective pressing triggers, and freedom for the elite attackers once the structure is secure. He is not a rigid single-system coach — across the 2022 World Cup win he used a back four in a 4-3-3/4-4-2 hybrid for most matches but switched to a back three (3-5-2/5-3-2 out of possession) specifically to match Netherlands' shape in the quarter-final. The recurring idea, repeated by his own staff, is: never let the front-line stars disconnect from the defensive structure. The team stays compact and disciplined in a mid-to-low block, then breaks with speed through Messi's clever positioning and central runners.

Scaloni's biggest tactical evolution during 2022 was reshaping midfield cover so a then-35-year-old Messi could be a free-roaming No. 10/false-nine hybrid rather than a wide chase-back winger — De Paul and the double pivot absorbed his defensive workload. That same logic now applies with even greater force at Messi's current age (39): his positional discipline off the ball is minimal by design, and the pivot must screen far more ground behind him.

## Formation Options

**Option A — 4-3-3 (base, possession-oriented control)**
- GK: Emiliano Martínez
- Back four: Nahuel Molina (RB) — Cristian Romero, Nicolás Otamendi (CBs) — Nicolás Tagliafico (LB)
- Pivot/double six: Alexis Mac Allister, Enzo Fernández
- No. 8 / free eight: Leandro Paredes or Giovani Lo Celso
- Front three: Nico Paz (right half-space, drifting inside) — Lautaro Martínez (central) — Lionel Messi (left, inverted, free role)

**Option B — 4-4-2 diamond (2022 World Cup base shape, direct/counter emphasis)**
- GK: Emiliano Martínez
- Back four: Molina — Romero — Lisandro Martínez — Tagliafico
- Diamond: De Paul (base of diamond, destroyer) — Mac Allister & Enzo Fernández (sides) — Messi (tip, free role behind the strikers)
- Front two: Lautaro Martínez + Julián Alvarez (mobile second striker, drops to combine and drags centre-backs)

**Option C — 3-5-2 / 5-3-2 (matched-up defensive shape, used for physically dominant or counter-heavy opponents, mirrors the Netherlands 2022 QF setup)**
- GK: Emiliano Martínez
- Back three: Romero — Otamendi — Lisandro Martínez, with Molina and Tagliafico as wing-backs
- Midfield three: Mac Allister — Enzo Fernández — De Paul
- Front two: Messi (dropping deep to link) + Lautaro Martínez

## Key-Player Dependencies

- **Messi (39, Inter Miami):** The single biggest tactical variable. Without him, Argentina loses its only guaranteed individual chance-creator in tight games and must generate more through combination play (Nico Paz, Almada, Enzo). His defensive contribution is already near zero and shrinking — expect him used in shorter match windows, rotated more aggressively than in 2022, with Nico Paz or Thiago Almada as the like-for-like creative deputy in the No. 10/left-forward zone. Tactically, the team is no longer built to lean on Messi's box-to-box running; it is built to get him the ball in the final third with minimal defensive cost, and to accept his 2018-era dribbling volume is gone in favor of vision and set-piece delivery.
- **Emiliano Martínez:** Undisputed starter; commanding, vocal, elite penalty-shootout record and sweeper-keeper distribution. If unavailable, drop-off to Rulli/Musso is significant in composure under pressure and distribution range.
- **Cristian Romero:** Aggressive, front-foot man-marking centre-back; his willingness to step out and duel is central to the high/mid block. Losing him forces a more passive back line.
- **Alexis Mac Allister:** The player who allows the double pivot to function box-to-box; his ball progression from deep is what lets Messi and the front line stay high without the team getting overrun in transition.
- **Enzo Fernández:** Range of passing and tempo control from deep; without him, build-up becomes more direct and less patient.
- **Lautaro Martínez:** Primary out-and-out finisher and pressing trigger up front; his off-ball running is what starts the front press.

## Pressing & Defensive Shape

Argentina defends in a mid-block by default, compact vertically, inviting the opponent into wide areas before pressing triggers spring around the touchline. High press is used selectively and in bursts (Lautaro/Alvarez as first pressers, De Paul or Enzo stepping to cut passing lanes), not sustained Klopp-style gegenpressing — the squad's average age and Messi's reduced work rate make 90 minutes of high-intensity pressing unrealistic. Out possession, the back line sits deeper than most European elite sides, prioritizing not being played through over compressing space high up the pitch.

## Set-Piece Notes (Internal)

Corners: near-post flick routines targeting Romero/Otamendi's aerial strength, with Lisandro Martínez as a secondary far-post threat despite lacking ideal height for the role. Messi and Paredes share dead-ball responsibility — Messi favored for direct free kicks in the D, Paredes/Enzo for deep deliveries into the box. Emiliano Martínez's penalty-shootout preparation (extensive scouting of opposing takers, gamesmanship) remains a genuine tournament weapon.

## Honest Tactical Weakness

The central defensive pairing is aging (Otamendi is 38) and the squad's core spine (Otamendi, Tagliafico, De Paul, Paredes, Messi himself) skews old for a World Cup cycle. Against high-tempo transition teams that can isolate the back line in space — particularly on the counter after Argentina commits full-backs forward — this is the clearest exploitable weakness. Pace in behind, especially down Otamendi's side, is the most repeatable way an opponent scouting this team should look to attack it.
