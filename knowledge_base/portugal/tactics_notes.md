# Portugal — Internal Tactical Dossier (Staff Use Only)

**Classification:** Internal / Coaching Staff — not for public release.
**Manager:** Roberto Martínez
**Honours under Martínez-era squad core:** UEFA European Championship 2016, UEFA Nations League 2019
**Context:** Post-match review following the 0-1 loss to Spain (2026-07-06, World Cup knockout stage).

## Martínez's Managerial Philosophy

Roberto Martínez is a possession-first, Cruyff-influenced coach. His football is built on patient circulation, positional structure (juego de posición), and technical comfort under pressure — traits carried over from his Swansea, Wigan, Everton, and Belgium spells. At Belgium he took a golden generation to a third-place finish at the 2018 World Cup by prioritising individual quality within a flexible shape rather than rigid pressing systems. His man-management is his strongest asset: he is known for building trust with senior stars (De Bruyne/Hazard at Belgium; Ronaldo here), giving them tactical freedom while asking the rest of the structure to compensate defensively. The risk of this approach — well documented by critics at Everton and with Belgium — is a tendency toward defensive fragility and slow reaction to being pressed high, since his sides prioritise ball retention over rest-defence organisation.

With Portugal, this has translated into a 4-3-3 as the primary base, with a back-three (3-4-2-1 / 3-4-3) as a secondary, more defensively conservative option against teams that punish Portugal in transition.

## Formation Options

**Option A — 4-3-3 (primary, possession-control):**
- GK: Diogo Costa
- Back four: Diogo Dalot (RB), Tomás Araújo (RCB), Rúben Dias (LCB), Nuno Mendes (LB)
- Midfield three: Rúben Neves (pivot), João Neves and Vitinha (interiors) — alternative: Bruno Fernandes as the more advanced 8
- Front three: Pedro Neto (RW), Gonçalo Ramos (CF), Rafael Leão (LW)

**Option B — 4-2-3-1 (used vs. deep blocks, maximises Bernardo/Bruno creativity):**
- GK: Diogo Costa
- Back four: Dalot, Rúben Dias, Tomás Araújo, Nuno Mendes
- Double pivot: Rúben Neves, João Neves
- Attacking midfield line: Bernardo Silva, Bruno Fernandes, Rafael Leão
- Lone striker: Gonçalo Ramos (or Cristiano Ronaldo in a "one last dance" selection for statement fixtures)

**Option C — 3-4-2-1 (defensive/control, used vs. quick transition teams like Spain):**
- GK: Diogo Costa
- Back three: Tomás Araújo, Rúben Dias, Renato Veiga
- Wing-backs: Diogo Dalot (R), Nuno Mendes (L)
- Double pivot: Rúben Neves, João Neves
- Attacking two: Bernardo Silva, Bruno Fernandes (or Vitinha)
- Striker: Rafael Leão (false 9) or Gonçalo Ramos

## Key-Player Dependencies

- **Rúben Neves (86):** the deepest pivot is the structural key. Without him, the back three loses its passing outlet under press and João Neves is forced to drop, thinning the midfield's forward presence. No like-for-like backup at his level.
- **Rúben Dias (85) / Tomás Araújo (87):** this central pairing is the spine. Araújo has emerged as arguably Portugal's most reliable ball-playing centre-back (87 internal rating, highest-rated outfield defender); losing either forces Renato Veiga or João Cancelo (used as an auxiliary CB in Cancelo's more recent career adaptation) into the middle, reducing aerial control.
- **Cristiano Ronaldo (41, 79):** at this stage of his career, Ronaldo is a super-sub / statement-fixture starter rather than a guaranteed 90-minute starter. His tactical value now is box presence, penalties, set-piece finishing, and dressing-room gravity rather than pressing or box-to-box work. The team's actual attacking axis has shifted to **Rafael Leão and Bernardo Silva** — Leão provides the in-behind pace Ronaldo no longer offers, Bernardo provides the tempo-setting dribble-and-combine play in tight areas. If Martínez starts Ronaldo, the front line must compensate defensively (Leão and the pivot dropping deeper to cover Ronaldo's reduced defensive work rate).
- **Nuno Mendes (61 rating but high ceiling):** the only genuine inverted/overlapping left-back with PSG-level tempo; without him the left side loses width and Renato Veiga or João Cancelo must be redeployed there, slowing build-up on that flank.
- **Diogo Costa (84):** distribution from the back is central to the possession model; a backup keeper (José Sá, Rui Silva) changes the tempo of build-up play noticeably.

## Pressing & Defensive Shape

Portugal press in a mid-to-high block, triggered by wide areas rather than a full man-oriented gegenpress — consistent with Martínez's preference for controlled pressing over chaotic counter-pressing. The front three/attacking line curve passing lanes centrally, inviting play wide, where Dalot and Mendes (or wing-backs in the back-three shape) close down aggressively. The known weakness: the double pivot (Neves/Neves or Neves/Vitinha) can be bypassed by direct, vertical passing into a mobile forward, since neither is a natural destroyer — both are progressive, technical profiles rather than out-and-out ball-winners. This mirrors long-standing criticism of Martínez sides (Everton, Belgium) being vulnerable to fast transitional play through the middle.

## Set-Piece Notes (Internal)

Attacking corners: near-post flick routine targeting Rúben Dias/Tomás Araújo's aerial strength, with Bruno Fernandes and Rúben Neves as delivery options (both strong dead-ball takers). Ronaldo remains a designated penalty-taker and far-post/edge-of-box option when on the pitch. Defensively, zonal-plus-man hybrid at corners has been inconsistent — a repeated source of concern given goals conceded from set plays in recent cycles.

## Loss to Spain (2026-07-06) — Internal Review

Spain's control of central midfield tempo repeatedly bypassed Portugal's double pivot, and the single goal conceded came from a transition sequence exploiting the gap left when Nuno Mendes stepped up to press. Portugal had spells of possession dominance (consistent with the Martínez model) but lacked a cutting edge in the final third — Ronaldo and Ramos were both isolated against a well-organised low block for long stretches. This is the honest, recurring weakness: **when the double pivot is bypassed and the back line is forced into 1v1 duels in transition, Portugal's rest-defence is thin**, since both full-backs are selected primarily for attacking quality rather than defensive discipline. An opponent that can commit numbers quickly in transition (as Spain did) remains our clearest tactical vulnerability heading into the knockout rounds.
