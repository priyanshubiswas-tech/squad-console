# Cape Verde — Internal Tactical Dossier (Staff Use Only)

**Classification:** Internal / Coaching Staff — not for public release.
**Manager:** Bubista (Pedro Leitão Brito)
**Honours under Bubista:** None major on the trophy shelf — the honour is the campaign itself: first-ever FIFA World Cup qualification (secured 13 Oct 2025, 3-0 vs. Eswatini, topping a group over Cameroon and Angola), and Bubista's CAF Coach of the Year 2025 award.
**Context:** Post-match review following the 3-2 extra-time loss to Argentina (2026-07-03), Round of 32.

## Bubista's Managerial Philosophy

Pedro Leitão Brito — known throughout Cape Verdean football simply as Bubista (after his home island, Boa Vista) — captained the national team for eleven years as a centre-back before moving into coaching. That defender's instinct still defines his management: defensive organisation first, discipline and unit cohesion second, individual flair a distant third. He took over in January 2020, led Cape Verde to the 2021 AFCON via a Mozambique play-off win, and has since built what is widely regarded as one of Africa's most cohesive national set-ups — no small feat given the squad is drawn almost entirely from a scattered European diaspora (Portugal, France, Turkey, MLS, and beyond) with limited time together outside camps. His stated philosophy is explicitly about proving that resource gaps don't have to be destiny: "As big as the opponent might be on the world stage, many national teams become equal." In practice this means Cape Verde rarely try to out-play anyone; they try to out-organise them, stay compact, and strike through moments — a set piece, a transition, an individual piece of quality — rather than sustained territorial dominance.

## Key-Player Dependencies

- **Vozinha (GK, 40):** the entire defensive plan is built around his shot-stopping — he faced 27 Spain shots and conceded nothing. He is also the emotional/captaincy anchor of the group. Neither CJ dos Santos nor Márcio Rosa has meaningful tournament-level experience as his replacement; a Vozinha injury would be the single most destabilising event available to an opponent's game-plan.
- **Logan Costa (CB, 25):** the squad's best individual defender and only player with genuine top-five-league pedigree (Villarreal); he only made the squad after recovering from an ACL injury. Without him, Pico is left marshalling a considerably less experienced pairing (Sidny Lopes Cabral, 23, or a makeshift Stopira recall at 38).
- **Kevin Pina (MF, 29):** the primary source of created danger from a team that otherwise generates very few clear chances in open play — his 30-yard free-kick against Uruguay is the clearest example of how goals actually arrive for this side. Set-piece delivery and distance shooting are disproportionately important to the attacking output.
- **Garry Rodrigues / Nuno da Costa:** the senior front pairing responsible for holding the ball up and converting the rare counter-attacking or set-piece chance created — against Saudi Arabia the team out-xG'd the opponent (1.52 to 0.40) but could not convert, underlining that finishing depends heavily on these two being sharp.
- **Sidny Lopes Cabral (CB/utility, 23):** the breakout name of the run — scored the stunning equaliser against Argentina in extra time — but also inadvertently headed a Uruguay delivery against his own post that led directly to Uruguay's first goal. High ceiling, still error-prone under aerial pressure.

## Pressing & Defensive Shape

Cape Verde defend in a compact mid-block, generally a 4-4-2 or 4-1-4-1 shape out of possession regardless of which attacking option was started in. Pressing triggers are specific rather than universal: backward passes into opposition full-backs, heavy/loose touches near the touchline, and central receptions where the receiving player has his back to goal. Rather than chasing the ball high, the front players are coached to show opponents into these low-value pass options and only then accelerate the press. In possession transition, the right-back is often instructed to invert into central midfield, forming a 3-2 rest-defence shape that has repeatedly denied stronger sides (Spain, Uruguay, Saudi Arabia) clean counter-attacking lanes. This is a mid-block/low-block hybrid, not a high press — patience and shape discipline over athleticism.

## Set-Piece Notes (Internal)

Attacking set pieces run through Kevin Pina, who is comfortable both delivering and shooting direct from range — his free-kick against Uruguay bypassed a two-man wall from 30 yards. Defensively, this is the clearest and most repeatable weakness in the squad: **two of the last three goals conceded (Uruguay's equaliser, headed in off a defensive rebound after Sidny Lopes Cabral's own-post header under pressure from a corner; and Argentina's extra-time winner, headed by Cristian Romero from a Messi corner and credited as an own goal off Diney)** came directly from set pieces. Zonal marking discipline in the six-yard box breaks down under sustained aerial pressure from technically superior, physically imposing opposition. Any opponent generating repeated corners should be considered a live goal threat regardless of open-play form.

## Loss to Argentina (2026-07-03, aet) — Internal Review

Cape Verde matched Argentina for long spells and led through Deroy Duarte's 59th-minute equaliser before Lautaro Martínez's 92nd-minute strike forced extra time, and Sidny Lopes Cabral's brilliant individual finish in the 103rd minute levelled it again before Cristian Romero's 111th-minute header — from yet another Messi corner — proved decisive. The honest, recurring weakness this exposes: **our compact, low-event gameplan is built to survive 90 minutes of pressure, not 120.** The squad skews old at exactly the positions that need fresh legs late (Stopira, 38; Vozinha, 40; Nuno da Costa, 35; Garry Rodrigues, 35; Ryan Mendes, 36), and once extra time began, the same zonal-marking fragility at corners that cost a goal against Uruguay resurfaced under Messi's delivery. Any knockout-stage opponent capable of taking this team to 120 minutes should be considered dangerous by default, and our set-piece defending needs specific in-camp attention before it happens again.
