# Brazil National Team — Internal Tactical Dossier

**CLASSIFICATION: INTERNAL STAFF USE ONLY — CBF Technical Committee**
Not for public or press distribution. Prepared for coaching staff, analysts, and federation technical directors ahead of the 2026 FIFA World Cup.

## Manager Profile: Carlo Ancelotti

Ancelotti was confirmed as head coach in May 2025, his first full-time international job after a club career spanning AC Milan, Chelsea, PSG, Bayern Munich, Napoli, Everton, and two spells at Real Madrid, where he won a record haul of Champions League titles. His defining trait as a manager has never been a fixed system — it is man-management. He builds the shape around the best available players rather than forcing players into a rigid idea, and he is famously willing to change formation mid-tournament if the group in the dressing room tells him to. Expect in-game flexibility between a back four and back three, and a willingness to sacrifice defensive solidity for attacking talent when the squad demands it (a pattern from his Real Madrid sides with Vinícius Júnior, Rodrygo, and Bellingham all accommodated simultaneously).

With Brazil, his early tenure has leaned on a **4-2-3-1** as the base structure, with a double pivot shielding the back line and a free No. 10 linking to a front three. He has also used a **4-3-3** for more control against possession-heavy opponents and has experimented with a **4-2-4** in must-win scenarios to maximize attacking talent. A core principle: once possession is won, Brazil look to verticalize immediately rather than recycle the ball patiently — quick line-breaking passes into the front players before the opponent can reorganize.

## Formation Options (Real Squad)

### Option A — 4-2-3-1 (base / balanced)
GK: Alisson (or Ederson Moraes) — CBs: Marquinhos, Bremer — FBs: Danilo Luiz (RB), Alex Sandro (LB) — DM pivot: Casemiro, Fabinho — AM: Lucas Paquetá — Wide forwards: Raphinha (R), Vinícius Júnior (L) — CF: Endrick or Igor Thiago.
This is the closest to Ancelotti's stated preference: double pivot protects an aging back line, Paquetá links midfield-to-attack, and Vinícius/Raphinha provide direct 1v1 threat in behind.

### Option B — 4-3-3 (control vs. possession teams)
GK: Alisson — CBs: Marquinhos, Roger Ibañez — FBs: Danilo Luiz, Douglas Santos — Midfield three: Casemiro (holding), Bruno Guimarães (box-to-box), Lucas Paquetá (advanced) — Front three: Raphinha, Luiz Henrique/Igor Thiago, Vinícius Júnior.
Used when facing a technically strong midfield (e.g., a rerun of the Argentina qualifying losses) where Brazil needs an extra body centrally rather than pure width.

### Option C — 4-2-4 (chase-the-game / max attacking talent)
GK: Ederson Moraes — CBs: Marquinhos, Bremer — FBs: Alex Sandro, Danilo Luiz — Double pivot: Fabinho, Danilo Santos — Front four: Raphinha, Vinícius Júnior, Endrick, Neymar (free role in the hole).
High-risk shape reserved for second-half chases; Neymar's positional freedom is the point — he drifts to find pockets while the double pivot absorbs transition risk.

## Key-Player Dependencies

- **Vinícius Júnior**: The single biggest tactical dependency. His value is almost entirely in isolation 1v1s on the left half-space/wing — explosive first step, cuts inside onto the right foot. Without him, Brazil loses its most reliable individual chance-creation source and Ancelotti has to manufacture combination play through Paquetá/Raphinha instead, which is slower to build and easier to defend as a bloc.
- **Neymar**: Now 34 and playing domestically at Santos; his tactical value has shifted from explosive dribbling to game intelligence — link play, disguised passing, dead-ball delivery, and drawing fouls in dangerous zones. If unavailable, Brazil loses its best set-piece taker and its only true "slow the game down" option when protecting a lead.
- **Casemiro**: The defensive midfield screen the entire back four is built around, especially with Marquinhos/Bremer stepping into midfield areas during buildup. Without him, both double-pivot alternatives (Fabinho alone, or Bruno Guimarães pushed deeper) leave more space in the half-spaces for opponents to counter into — this was visible against Norway.
- **Marquinhos**: Organizes the back line and is comfortable stepping into midfield to progress the ball, which is central to Ancelotti's build-out. Bremer and Roger Ibañez are both quality but lack Marquinhos' passing range under pressure.

## Pressing & Defensive Shape

Brazil under Ancelotti do not press as a high, coordinated block for 90 minutes — the approach is closer to selective, triggered pressing (front players press the ball when a pass is misplaced or a touch is heavy), dropping into a mid-block otherwise. This suits an aging back line (Alex Sandro, 35; Danilo Luiz, 34) that cannot cover in-behind space repeatedly. The risk: if the front line's press is beaten (e.g., by a team playing quick one-touch combinations through midfield), the gap between the front three/four and the double pivot becomes exploitable, since neither Casemiro nor Fabinho has the recovery pace of a decade ago.

## What Went Wrong vs. Norway (1-2, World Cup, 2026-07-05)

Internal review points to three factors:
1. **Transition defending** — Norway's direct, physical approach exploited the exact gap described above: once Brazil lost the ball in the middle third, the double pivot was not compact enough to stop the first pass forward, and the back line (particularly the full-backs pushed high in the 4-2-3-1) was caught square.
2. **Set-piece vulnerability** — Norway's height advantage at both boxes was not adequately scouted; Brazil's zonal/man mix at corners left a free header inside the six-yard box for the second goal.
3. **Lack of a control option in midfield** — with Bruno Guimarães used sparingly, Brazil had no one to slow the tempo once behind, leading to rushed, low-quality final third decisions.
Recommendation: consider Option B (4-3-3 with Bruno Guimarães) against physically dominant, direct opponents rather than the 4-2-3-1.

## Exploitable Weakness (Opponent Scouting Angle)

The starting full-back pairing (Alex Sandro, 35, and Danilo Luiz, 34) is the clearest point of attack for opponents: both are positionally sound but have lost a step of recovery pace. Sustained wide overloads with a fast inverted winger isolating either full-back 1v1, combined with quick switches of play before Brazil's back line can shift across, is the most repeatable route to chances against this side.

## Squad Depth & Rotation

- **Goalkeepers**: Alisson (first choice on reputation/experience) — Ederson Moraes (strong distribution, viable starter) — Weverton (veteran cover, limited to emergency minutes).
- **Center-backs**: Marquinhos and Bremer as the first pairing; Roger Ibañez as first man off the bench (mobile, comfortable in a back three); Gabriel Magalhães and Léo Pereira as further depth/back-three options.
- **Full-backs**: Danilo Luiz and Alex Sandro start; Douglas Santos is the direct like-for-like backup on the left, offering more overlapping energy.
- **Defensive midfield**: Casemiro undisputed starter; Fabinho as both backup and 4-2-3-1 partner; Danilo Santos as a younger, more mobile depth option.
- **Attacking midfield/wide**: Lucas Paquetá and Bruno Guimarães rotate the No. 10/8 role depending on whether Ancelotti wants creativity or control; Raphinha is first-choice right wing.
- **Forwards**: Vinícius Júnior and Endrick are first-choice picks in the front line; Luiz Henrique and Igor Thiago provide different profiles (Luiz Henrique for wide dribbling threat, Igor Thiago as a target-man No. 9 alternative to Endrick); Neymar and Matheus Cunha offer experience/versatility off the bench; Gabriel Martinelli and Rayan are development-squad depth for injury cover.

## Individual Player Notes

- **Vinícius Júnior** (LW, Real Madrid): Left-footed, elite acceleration and change of direction, primarily a cut-inside dribbler from the left; defensively will track back but is not a natural presser.
- **Raphinha** (RW, Barcelona): Left-footed inverted winger who can also play off the right naturally, strong end product, presses aggressively from the front, comfortable in combination play.
- **Neymar** (FW, Santos): Two-footed, elite close control and disguise, now more of a creator/set-piece specialist than a repeated dribbler at 34; needs the game to come to him rather than pressing high.
- **Endrick** (CF, Lyon): Right-footed, still just 19; instinctive penalty-box finisher, makes sharp runs in behind, less involved in deep buildup.
- **Casemiro** (DM, Manchester United): Right-footed, positionally excellent screen in front of the back four, strong in the air at both boxes, aerial threat at set pieces; lost a yard of pace which shows against quick transitions.
- **Marquinhos** (CB, PSG): Right-footed, calm under pressure, comfortable carrying the ball into midfield, the vocal organizer of the back line.
- **Bremer** (CB, Juventus): Right-footed, physical, strong in duels and in the air, less comfortable in extended possession sequences than Marquinhos.
- **Alex Sandro** (LB, Flamengo): Left-footed, experienced and positionally sound, but at 35 his recovery speed is the squad's clearest defensive liability.
- **Bruno Guimarães** (CM, Newcastle): Left-footed, excellent range of passing and tempo control, capable of both a No. 6 and No. 8 role.
- **Alisson** (GK, Liverpool): Elite shot-stopper and a genuine passing outlet from the back, comfortable sweeping behind a high line.

## Set-Piece Notes (Internal)

Corners: primary routine targets the near-post run with a blocker set to free a central header (Bremer/Marquinhos as aerial targets); short-corner variation used to draw markers and create a cutback lane for a first-time strike. Free kicks in the final third: Neymar and Raphinha share delivery duties depending on foot/side; direct free kicks around the box are Neymar's first option when fit. Defensively, the Norway match exposed that the zonal setup at the near post is not currently matched to opponents with a genuine aerial target — this is flagged for correction before the knockout stage.
