# Tactical Theory — Shared Reference

A concept glossary the analyst can draw on for any team, regardless of which squad is active. Every per-team `tactics_notes.md` and `public_scouting.md` assumes familiarity with these terms rather than re-explaining them — this file is what the RAG retriever pulls in as `shared_theory` alongside team-specific documents.

## Formations

- **4-3-3**: four defenders, three central midfielders, three forwards (usually one central striker flanked by two wide forwards who invert or hug the touchline). Favours control through midfield numbers and width from advanced wingers. Vulnerable to being overloaded centrally if the front three doesn't track back, since it leaves only three central midfielders against a false nine or double-pivot buildup.
- **4-2-3-1**: two holding midfielders shield the back four, one attacking midfielder plays between the lines, three forwards (a central striker plus two wide players who can be wingers or inverted forwards). Balances defensive solidity (two pivots) with a creative outlet in the #10 zone. The lone striker can become isolated if service from midfield is slow.
- **4-4-2**: two banks of four plus two strikers. Compact and simple to organize defensively (two clear horizontal lines), historically favoured by counter-attacking sides. Can be outnumbered in central midfield (2 v 3) against teams playing a back three or 4-3-3.
- **3-5-2** / **5-3-2**: three central defenders, wing-backs providing width in both boxes, two strikers. Wing-backs are the key variable — going forward it's effectively a back three with attacking width; defending deep it becomes a back five. Physically demanding on the wing-back role across 90 minutes.
- **False nine**: a nominal striker who drops deep into midfield to draw a marking center-back out of position, creating space in behind for wingers or attacking midfielders to run into. Disrupts man-marking defenses more than zonal ones.

## Pressing and defensive shape

- **High press / gegenpressing**: winning the ball back immediately after losing it, high up the pitch, before the opponent can organize. Demands high fitness and a high defensive line (offside trap risk). Effective against teams who build out slowly from the back.
- **Mid-block**: pressing triggers start around the halfway line rather than in the opponent's third — a compromise between compactness and pressure, common against technically superior opposition.
- **Low block**: the whole team sits deep (often close to its own box), inviting pressure and looking to break quickly on turnovers. Minimizes space in behind at the cost of ceding territory and possession.
- **Offside trap**: a high defensive line steps forward in a coordinated line just before the ball is played, aiming to catch an attacker offside. High risk/reward — a single defender mistiming the step gifts a clear run on goal.

## Buildup and possession concepts

- **Double pivot**: two central midfielders sitting side by side just in front of the back four, splitting buildup and defensive-cover duties between them. Common in 4-2-3-1 and some 4-3-3 variants.
- **Overloading a flank**: committing extra players (a fullback, winger, and a midfielder drifting wide) to one side to create a numerical advantage, typically to isolate a weaker opposition fullback in a 1v1 or 2v1.
- **Inverted fullback**: a fullback who steps into central midfield during buildup rather than staying wide, adding a passing option centrally and helping control transitions when possession is lost.
- **Third-man runs**: a passing pattern where a player between the ball-carrier and an advanced teammate plays a first-time pass to release that teammate, bypassing a defensive line that was set to intercept a direct pass.

## Set pieces

- **Zonal vs. man marking (corners)**: zonal marking assigns each defender a patch of the six-yard box regardless of which attacker enters it; man marking assigns each defender a specific attacker to track everywhere. Most modern sides use a hybrid — zonal cover on the front and back posts plus specific man-markers on the opposition's aerial threats.
- **Near-post flick-on**: a corner or free kick aimed at the near post for a flick header across goal, exploiting the split-second before defenders can react to a shorter delivery than a traditional far-post cross.

## Reading a fixture

When comparing two teams, the useful axes are: buildup style (how they progress the ball from the back), pressing trigger (where they start applying pressure), and out-of-possession shape (which formation they defend in — often different from their attacking shape, e.g. a 4-3-3 in possession collapsing into a 4-5-1 without it). A tactical mismatch usually shows up as one team's buildup strength lining up directly against the other's defensive weakness — e.g. a side that builds through wide overloads against a team whose fullbacks are its weakest defensive link.
